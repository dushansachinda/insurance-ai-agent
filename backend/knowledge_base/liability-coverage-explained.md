# Liability Coverage Explained

Liability coverage is the foundation of every auto insurance policy. It pays for the damage and injuries you cause to other people when you're at fault in an accident — and unlike collision or comprehensive, it's required by law in nearly every U.S. state.

## The Two Parts of Liability

Auto liability is split into two distinct coverages:

- **Bodily injury liability (BI)**: Pays the medical bills, lost wages, and pain-and-suffering claims of people you injure. It also pays your legal defense if you're sued.
- **Property damage liability (PD)**: Pays to repair or replace the other party's car, fence, mailbox, building, or any other property you damage.

It's important to understand that liability coverage never pays for your own injuries or your own vehicle damage. Those losses are covered by other parts of your policy: medical payments / personal injury protection, and collision / comprehensive.

## Reading Liability Limits

Liability limits are usually written as three numbers, like 50/100/50. These represent thousands of dollars:

- **First number (50)**: bodily injury limit per person.
- **Second number (100)**: bodily injury limit per accident, total.
- **Third number (50)**: property damage limit per accident.

So a 50/100/50 policy will pay up to $50,000 for one injured person, up to $100,000 total across everyone hurt in the accident, and up to $50,000 for property damage.

## State Minimums vs. Recommended Limits

Every state sets a minimum liability limit, but those minimums are often dangerously low — sometimes as little as 25/50/25. A serious accident can easily produce six-figure medical bills, and once your liability runs out, the injured party can sue you personally for the rest. Most insurance professionals recommend at least 100/300/100, and higher if you have meaningful assets to protect.

## Umbrella Policies

If you want protection above and beyond your auto liability limits, an umbrella policy adds an extra layer — typically $1 million or more — that kicks in once your underlying auto and home liability limits are exhausted. Umbrella coverage is surprisingly affordable for the protection it offers.

Liability coverage doesn't repair your car, but it can save your savings, your home, and your future income from a single bad afternoon on the road.
