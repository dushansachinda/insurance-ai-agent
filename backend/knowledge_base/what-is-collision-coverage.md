# What Is Collision Coverage?

Collision coverage is the part of your auto insurance policy that pays to repair or replace your own vehicle when it is damaged in a collision with another vehicle or object. Unlike liability coverage, which protects other people and their property, collision coverage focuses entirely on your car.

## When Collision Coverage Applies

Collision coverage typically kicks in when you:

- Hit another vehicle, regardless of who is at fault.
- Strike a stationary object such as a guardrail, fence, mailbox, or tree.
- Roll your vehicle in a single-car accident.
- Are involved in a hit-and-run where the other driver cannot be identified (in some states this falls under collision rather than uninsured motorist).

It does not apply to weather damage, theft, vandalism, or hitting an animal — those events fall under comprehensive coverage.

## How Payouts Work

If you file a collision claim, the insurer estimates the cost of repairs. You pay your deductible (the amount you agreed to cover yourself, often $500 or $1,000), and the insurance company pays the remainder up to the actual cash value of your vehicle. If repair costs exceed the car's value, the vehicle is declared a total loss and you receive a payout equal to its market value, minus the deductible.

## Is Collision Coverage Required?

Collision coverage is not legally required in any U.S. state. However, if you finance or lease your vehicle, your lender almost always requires it until the loan is paid off. Once you own the vehicle outright, collision becomes optional, and many drivers of older cars choose to drop it because the premium can outweigh the maximum possible payout.

## Choosing the Right Deductible

A higher deductible lowers your premium but increases your out-of-pocket cost after an accident. Common choices are $250, $500, $1,000, and $1,500. Pick a number you could comfortably pay on short notice if the worst happened tomorrow.

Collision coverage provides peace of mind: knowing that even a serious wreck won't leave you scrambling to afford repairs is a meaningful safety net for most drivers.
