# How Deductibles Work

A deductible is the amount you agree to pay out of pocket before your insurance company starts paying on a claim. It's one of the most important numbers on your policy because it affects both your premium and what happens after an accident.

## A Simple Example

Imagine you have a $500 deductible on your collision coverage. You're rear-ended in a parking lot and the repair estimate is $3,200. After you file a claim:

- You pay the first $500 to the repair shop (or to yourself, if you handle the repair).
- Your insurer pays the remaining $2,700.

If the repair had cost only $400 — less than your deductible — there would be no payout from the insurer, and filing a claim would not make sense.

## Per-Claim, Not Per-Year

Auto deductibles are charged per claim, not per policy year. If you have two separate accidents in the same year, you'll pay the deductible twice. This differs from health insurance, where most deductibles reset annually.

## How the Deductible Affects Your Premium

Insurers offer a discount when you choose a higher deductible because you're agreeing to absorb more of the small claims yourself. Typical options:

- $250 deductible: highest premium, lowest out-of-pocket cost when filing.
- $500 deductible: a common middle-ground choice.
- $1,000 deductible: lower premium, larger bill if you have an accident.
- $1,500 deductible: lowest premium, but a significant out-of-pocket hit.

## Choosing the Right Deductible

Pick a deductible based on what you could comfortably pay on short notice. A useful rule of thumb: choose the highest deductible you could pay tomorrow without going into debt or draining an emergency fund. The premium savings over years often outweigh the larger one-time cost.

## Separate Deductibles for Collision and Comprehensive

Most policies let you set different deductibles for collision and comprehensive coverage. Many drivers choose a higher collision deductible (since collisions are usually their fault) and a lower comprehensive deductible (since weather and theft feel less controllable).

Understanding your deductible before something goes wrong helps you file claims with confidence and avoid surprises during an already stressful time.
